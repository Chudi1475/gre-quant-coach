# Question-Type Attack Playbook & Section Strategy

# GRE Quant Attack Playbook

> One rule above all: **accuracy on Section 1 is the score**. Everything below serves that.

---

## 1. The Four Question Types

### Quantitative Comparison (QC)
*Compare Quantity A vs B. Four fixed answers: (A) A greater, (B) B greater, (C) equal, (D) cannot be determined. QC always opens each section.*

**Decision procedure:**
1. If both quantities are pure numbers (no variables), the answer is **never (D)** — eliminate it instantly, then compute or estimate.
2. If variables exist, do not solve — **plug in the kill-set: 0, 1, a negative, a fraction (0–1), and a large number.**
3. After one plug-in, ask "what input would FLIP this?" Hunt the counterexample on purpose. If you find one flip, the answer is **(D)**. If no input can flip it, pick A/B/C.
4. Manipulate both columns identically (add/subtract anything; multiply/divide only by a known-positive) to simplify before plugging.

**Biggest trap:** assuming a variable is a positive integer. The single most punished mistake in QC is testing only "nice" numbers (2, 3, 5) and missing that **0, negatives, and fractions reverse the result.** A fraction between 0 and 1 gets *smaller* when squared and *bigger* when you take its root — opposite of integers. That one fact decides a huge share of (D) answers.

**Time budget:** **30–60 sec.** These are the cheapest points on the test. Bank time here for the marked problems later.

---

### Multiple Choice — One Answer
*Five options, pick exactly one.*

**Decision procedure:**
1. Read the question, then **read the answer choices before solving** — their spread tells you whether to estimate or compute exactly.
2. Choose the fastest legal path:
   - **Back-solve** (plug answers in, start with the middle value C) when the question asks for a specific unknown and choices are numbers.
   - **Plug in your own numbers** when choices contain variables.
   - **Estimate** when choices are far apart — round aggressively and pick the survivor.
   - Solve forward only when the algebra is genuinely faster than the above.
3. Sanity-check magnitude: is your answer in a believable range?

**Biggest trap:** the **"answered a different question" trap** — choices are seeded with the value you get one step early (e.g., they ask for *x+2* and "x" is sitting right there as a choice; they ask for the *increase* and put the *new total* as a choice). Re-read the last line of the prompt before you bubble.

**Time budget:** **60–90 sec.**

---

### Multiple Choice — Select All (one or more correct, NO partial credit)
*Box-style answers; could be one, several, or all correct.*

**Decision procedure:**
1. Treat each option as its own **independent true/false question** — never "pick the best."
2. Evaluate every single box. You must affirmatively judge each one.
3. When a range is involved (inequalities, "which could be the value of x"), **test the boundary/extreme cases**, not the middle — that's where right and wrong split.
4. No back-solving shortcut exists; you have to verify each.

**Biggest trap:** **stopping early.** You find two clear winners and move on, leaving a third correct box unchecked — and because there is zero partial credit, a 4-of-5 effort scores exactly the same as random: **wrong.** This type is the #1 silent score-killer. Force yourself to touch every box.

**Time budget:** **90–120 sec.** This is the one type you are *allowed* to spend on. Slow down here — the all-or-nothing scoring makes carefulness the highest-ROI behavior on the test.

---

### Numeric Entry
*Type the answer; sometimes a fraction in a two-box numerator/denominator.*

**Decision procedure:**
1. There are no choices to back-solve from, so you must solve cleanly — but you also don't need to simplify fractions (the box accepts any equivalent form).
2. Before typing, run the **FUR check: Form, Units, Rounding.**
   - **Form:** does it want a fraction, decimal, percent, or dollars?
   - **Units:** answer in minutes vs hours? cents vs dollars? meters vs cm?
   - **Rounding:** "to the nearest integer," "two decimal places" — obey exactly.
3. Negative answers and decimals are allowed unless stated otherwise.

**Biggest trap:** a **correct number in the wrong form/unit** — right math, but you typed 0.25 when it wanted 25 (percent), or 90 minutes when it wanted 1.5 (hours). No answer choices means no safety net to catch the format. The FUR check is non-negotiable.

**Time budget:** **75–100 sec.**

## 2. The Two-Pass Section Approach

Run every Quant section in exactly two passes:

**Pass 1 — Sweep (aim to finish with time banked):**
- Answer everything that is **quick and certain.** QCs, easy MCs, anything you see the path to in seconds.
- The instant a problem feels slow, murky, or computation-heavy: **put a placeholder guess, mark it, and move on.** Never sink two minutes early — early time is the most expensive time on the clock.
- Goal: reach the end of the section having captured all the easy points and banked minutes.

**Pass 2 — Hunt (spend the banked time):**
- Return only to marked items. Now you have minutes to spare and no pressure of unseen questions ahead.
- Work them hardest-value-first.

**Two hard rules:**
- **The 2-minute trip-wire:** if any single problem crosses ~2 minutes, mark it and leave — even mid-solve. Sunk cost will wreck your pacing.
- **Never leave a blank.** No wrong-answer penalty exists. Before time expires, fill *every* box, even with a pure guess. A blank and a wrong answer cost the same, so a guess is free upside.

Pace anchors: ~90 sec/question on the 12-Q section (21 min), ~92 sec/question on the 15-Q section (26 min). Average ~1.75 min.

## 3. Why Section 1 Is the #1 Lever — and How to Protect It

**The mechanism:** The GRE Quant is **section-level adaptive.** Section 1 is fixed at moderate difficulty. Your performance on Section 1 — difficulty handled *and* accuracy — does two things simultaneously:

1. It **selects which Section 2 you receive** (easier, medium, or hard).
2. It **sets the scoring ceiling.** The hard Section 2 is the only path to the top of the 130–170 scale. If you underperform Section 1, you are routed to an easier Section 2 whose maximum possible score is **capped below 170 no matter how perfectly you answer it.**

So Section 2 brilliance cannot rescue a weak Section 1 — the ceiling is already locked. Section 1 accuracy is the gate to every high point. This is why it is the single highest-leverage action on the entire test.

**Exactly how to protect Section 1:**
- **Spend your banked-time budget here, not in Section 2.** Trade a little speed for accuracy early. It is correct to finish Section 1 with less margin than Section 2 if it buys you clean answers.
- **Double-check the cheap types** (QC, easy MC) before bubbling — these are your guaranteed points and the foundation of unlocking hard Section 2.
- **Apply the kill-set and FUR checks rigorously in Section 1**, even when a problem looks obvious. The obvious-looking trap is exactly the calibrated mistake.
- **Run the two-pass approach inside Section 1 too**, but bias toward thoroughness on pass 2 — a single careless Section 1 miss can cost more than several Section 2 misses because of the ceiling effect.
- **Calm > fast.** The instinct to "save time for later" is backwards here. Section 1 is where time converts directly into your maximum achievable score. Protect it like the score depends on it — because it does.
