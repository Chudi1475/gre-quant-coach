# L01 — Fractions, decimals, and benchmark conversions

_Area: Arithmetic · Topic: fractions_decimals_


## Concept

## What a fraction actually is

A fraction is a division problem and a part-of-a-whole, at the same time. `3/4` literally means "3 divided by 4" AND "3 out of 4 equal pieces." Both readings are true and you'll switch between them constantly.

The **bottom number (denominator)** tells you how many equal pieces the whole is cut into. The **top number (numerator)** tells you how many of those pieces you have. Bigger denominator = smaller pieces. That's the whole game: `1/8` is a smaller slice than `1/3`, because cutting a pizza into 8 makes thinner slices than cutting it into 3.

## Fractions, decimals, percents are the same thing wearing different clothes

`1/2 = 0.5 = 50%`. One half, five tenths, fifty per hundred — identical quantity, three notations. The GRE constantly hands you one form and rewards you for instantly seeing the others. A "percent" is just a fraction with a fixed denominator of 100 (`per cent` = per hundred). A decimal is just a fraction whose denominator is a power of 10 (`0.375 = 375/1000`).

Fluency means you never stop to "compute" the common ones — you *recognize* them, the way you recognize a word without sounding out letters. That recognition is worth real seconds on every quantitative question.

## The four operations, intuitively

**Multiplying** fractions is the easy one: multiply straight across, top times top and bottom times bottom. `2/3 × 4/5 = 8/15`. Intuition: "two-thirds *of* four-fifths" — the word "of" means multiply. Taking a part of a part always gives you something smaller, which is why multiplying two proper fractions shrinks the number.

**Dividing** by a fraction = multiplying by its flip (reciprocal). `6 ÷ (1/2) = 6 × 2 = 12`, because "how many halves fit in 6?" is 12. This is the single most counterintuitive rule on the test: dividing by a number less than 1 makes things *bigger*. Keep-Change-Flip: keep the first, change ÷ to ×, flip the second.

**Adding and subtracting** is the slow one because the pieces must be the same size first. You can't add `1/2 + 1/3` directly — half-slices and third-slices aren't the same size. Convert both to a common denominator (here, sixths: `3/6 + 2/6 = 5/6`), then add the tops and keep the bottom. Never add denominators.

## Comparing without dividing

To compare `a/b` vs `c/d`, **cross-multiply**: compare `a×d` against `c×b`. Whichever product is bigger sits over the bigger fraction. `3/7` vs `4/9`: `3×9 = 27` vs `4×7 = 28`. The 28 belongs to `4/9`, so `4/9` is bigger. No decimals, no calculator. (This works because you're just clearing both denominators by the same positive amount.)

Build this until it's automatic. Almost every "which is greater" fraction question on the GRE dies instantly to cross-multiplication or to a benchmark you've memorized.


## Formulas that earn points

- **Multiply:** `(a/b) × (c/d) = (a×c)/(b×d)` — *when:* "of," scaling, chained fractions. Multiply across, simplify after.
- **Divide (Keep-Change-Flip):** `(a/b) ÷ (c/d) = (a/b) × (d/c)` — *when:* dividing by any fraction; complex fractions (a fraction over a fraction).
- **Add/Subtract:** `(a/b) ± (c/d) = (ad ± cb)/(bd)` — *when:* combining unlike fractions. Common denominator first, then add tops only.
- **Cross-multiply to compare:** `a/b > c/d` ⟺ `ad > cb` (denominators positive) — *when:* "which is greater" with two fractions, no calculator.
- **Fraction → percent:** `a/b × 100%` — *when:* converting; or just memorize the benchmarks below.
- **Decimal → fraction:** put digits over the right power of 10, then simplify. `0.4 = 4/10 = 2/5` — *when:* a decimal answer needs to be a clean fraction (Numeric Entry).
- **Percent change:** `(new − old)/old × 100%` — *when:* increase/decrease problems (foreshadows the percents lesson).


## Mental-math / speed

**Memorize these cold (this is the highest-ROI table in Arithmetic):**

| Fraction | Decimal | Percent |
|---|---|---|
| 1/2 | 0.5 | 50% |
| 1/3 | 0.333… | 33⅓% |
| 2/3 | 0.666… | 66⅔% |
| 1/4 | 0.25 | 25% |
| 3/4 | 0.75 | 75% |
| 1/5 | 0.2 | 20% |
| 1/8 | 0.125 | 12.5% |
| 3/8 | 0.375 | 37.5% |
| 5/8 | 0.625 | 62.5% |
| 1/6 | 0.1666… | 16⅔% |
| 1/9 | 0.111… | 11.1…% |
| 1/10 | 0.1 | 10% |

**Eighths shortcut:** each eighth is 0.125. So `n/8 = n × 0.125`. `7/8 = 7 × 0.125 = 0.875`.

**Ninths shortcut:** `n/9 = 0.nnn…` (repeating). `4/9 = 0.444…`, `7/9 = 0.777…`.

**"Of" = multiply, and reorder for ease.** `3/8 of 56`: divide first (56/8 = 7), then multiply (7 × 3 = 21). Dividing before multiplying keeps numbers small and avoids the calculator.

**Spot the equal-to-1 trick:** any fraction where top = bottom equals 1. `5/5`, `12/12`. And a fraction slightly less than 1 (like `7/8`) times a number is slightly less than that number — great for estimating and killing wrong answer choices.

**Benchmark anchoring for comparisons:** is `7/13` more or less than `1/2`? Half of 13 is 6.5, and 7 > 6.5, so `7/13 > 1/2`. Compare any fraction to 1/2 by doubling the numerator and checking against the denominator.


## Worked examples


**Example 1.** What is 3/8 of 56?

*Fast method:* Don't multiply 3 × 56 first. Divide by the denominator first because 56 is clean: 56 ÷ 8 = 7. Then × 3 = 21. Done in two mental steps, no calculator. (Reordering 'divide then multiply' keeps every intermediate number tiny.)

*Trap:* A rushed student computes 3 × 56 = 168 first, then has to do 168 ÷ 8 — uglier division, more error-prone, and tempts a reach for the calculator. Same answer, slower and riskier.


**Example 2.** Order these from least to greatest: 5/8, 7/11, 0.63.

*Fast method:* Convert everything to decimals using memorized benchmarks. 5/8 = 0.625 (eighths: 5 × 0.125). 0.63 stays. 7/11: 7/11 is just over 7/10.5 ≈ 0.636, and you can confirm it's bigger than 0.63 because 11 × 0.63 = 6.93 < 7. So order is 5/8 (0.625) < 0.63 < 7/11 (0.636).

*Trap:* Eyeballing 5/8 as 'a bit more than half, so probably biggest' and ranking by raw look. The numbers cluster near 0.63, so you MUST get precise. Memorizing 5/8 = 0.625 makes the whole comparison instant instead of a guess.


**Example 3.** Compute 1/2 + 1/3 − 1/4.

*Fast method:* Common denominator is 12 (LCM of 2, 3, 4). Convert: 6/12 + 4/12 − 3/12 = 7/12. As a decimal that's about 0.583. One pass, no calculator.

*Trap:* Adding tops AND bottoms: 1/2 + 1/3 'becomes' 2/5. Totally wrong — 2/5 = 0.4 is even smaller than 1/2. You can never add denominators; the slices must be resized to a common size first.

## Answers, fast solutions & traps


**P1 — 3/8**  
Solution: 0.375 = 375/1000. Both divide by 125: 375/125 = 3, 1000/125 = 8, giving 3/8. Or recognize it instantly from the benchmark table: 0.375 = 3/8.  
Trap: Stopping at 375/1000 or a partial reduction like 75/200. Numeric Entry wants lowest terms — always reduce fully before locking it in.


**P2 — C**  
Solution: 2/5 = 2 ÷ 5 = 0.4. Identical value, just different clothing. They're equal.  
Trap: Assuming a fraction and a decimal that 'look different' must differ in size, and picking A or B. Convert to one form and they're the same number.


**P3 — C**  
Solution: 'Of' = multiply, and divide first to keep numbers small. 1/4 of 240 = 60. Then 5/6 of 60: 60 ÷ 6 = 10, × 5 = 50.  
Trap: Multiplying 5 × 240 = 1200 up front and then wrestling with 1200 ÷ 24. Same answer, but ugly arithmetic invites mistakes and the calculator. Cancel and divide early.


**P4 — B**  
Solution: Cross-multiply: 3 × 9 = 27 sits over 3/7; 4 × 7 = 28 sits over 4/9. The bigger product, 28, belongs to 4/9, so Quantity B is greater. (Both are just under 1/2, and 4/9 = 0.444 edges out 3/7 = 0.428.)  
Trap: Guessing that the fraction with the bigger numbers (4/9) or the smaller numbers (3/7) is automatically bigger. Neither rule exists — you must cross-multiply or convert.


**P5 — A,B,C,E**  
Solution: 0.625 = 5/8 (benchmark), so A is in. B: 25/40 reduces by 5 to 5/8 — in. C: 0.6 + 0.025 = 0.625 — in. E: 15/24 reduces by 3 to 5/8 — in. D: 5/9 = 0.555… ≠ 0.625 — out. Answer: A, B, C, E.  
Trap: Stopping after you find one or two matches (MC-select has no partial credit, so you MUST test every option), or misreading C as 0.6 + 0.25 = 0.85. Evaluate each choice independently and to the end.


**P6 — 11/8**  
Solution: Top: common denominator 12 → 9/12 + 2/12 = 11/12. Bottom: 1 − 1/3 = 2/3. Now divide, Keep-Change-Flip: 11/12 × 3/2 = 33/24 = 11/8 (divide by 3). As a decimal, 1.375.  
Trap: Dividing by 2/3 by making the number smaller. Dividing by a value less than 1 makes the result LARGER (11/8 > 11/12). Also forgetting to find common denominators before combining the top, or skipping the final reduction of 33/24.


## Common mistakes

1. **Adding denominators.** `1/2 + 1/3 ≠ 2/5`. You can only add numerators after the denominators match. Always find a common denominator first; the slices must be the same size before you can count them.

2. **Thinking dividing always shrinks.** Dividing by a number less than 1 makes the result *bigger* (`6 ÷ 1/2 = 12`). Burn in Keep-Change-Flip and sanity-check: "how many halves fit in 6?"

3. **Not reducing on Numeric Entry.** The test wants lowest terms or the cleanest form. `375/1000` is correct in value but will be marked wrong as an answer — always simplify fully and check whether it wants a fraction, decimal, or percent.

4. **Quitting MC-select early.** No partial credit means a half-checked list scores zero. Evaluate every single option independently, all the way through, even after you've found a match.

5. **Eyeballing close comparisons.** When fractions/decimals cluster (0.62 vs 0.625 vs 0.636), intuition fails. Cross-multiply or convert to decimals using your memorized benchmark table — never rank by 'which looks bigger.'
